## AI-based detection and tracking of individual animal interactions using camera-trap.
#### PhD in Computer science at the University of Montpellier (January 2024 - December 2026).

**Keywords**: Deep learning, Computer vision, Animal re-identification, Dog re-identification datasets, Domain generalization, Wildlife monitoring, Camera-trap.

[My thesis](https://theses.fr/s381658) is part of the [SEAdogSEA](https://hal.science/hal-04637825), an interdisciplinary project between french and asian research institutions. The goal of the project is to study the contact patterns between dogs and their habitat to better comprehend their behavior and role as zoonotic disease transmitters. To this end, camera-traps were installed across three Asian countries, more specifically, Indonesia, Thailand and Cambodia. The thesis focuses on the development of an AI-based pipeline to automate the analysis of the large volumes of data generated by the camera-traps, with a particular focus on re-identification.

Please check the resume of the thesis below and our [publications](https://eugeniodias5.github.io/publications/). 

### Resume of the thesis
Studying animals' individual interactions in their natural environment provides crucial insight into behavior and disease transmission dynamics, enabling more effective conservation and epidemiological control strategies, including better-targeted vaccination campaigns and the identification of potential "super-spreader" individuals. However, the analysis of such interactions from video monitoring systems requires processing large volumes of data, which remains a major bottleneck. This thesis develops an AI-based pipeline to automate this process. We design an end-to-end system composed of three main stages: detection, multi-object tracking, and re-identification (Re-ID). Our research mainly focuses on the Re-ID step, which is the least studied in the literature. Unlike most state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods, which assume controlled conditions, we target real-world Re-ID, where the environment is uncontrolled and characterized by strong viewpoint variations, occlusions, background clutter, and temporal changes.

We develop and validate our approach in the context of dogs captured by camera traps, a setting of high epidemiological, while designing methods that extend naturally to quadrupeds in general. Our first contribution tackles the lack of suitable datasets. We release two valuable resources to the scientific community: the YT-BB-Dog, a short-term dataset of more than 2,700 dogs automatically extracted from YouTube videos and currently the largest dataset for dog Re-ID, and the SEAdogSEA-Indonesia, a long-term, cross-camera dataset comprising 183 dogs recorded through 47 camera traps over four sessions spanning three years in a small village in Indonesia, enriched with geographical and socio-ecological metadata validated by both specialists and the dogs' owners.

Building on these datasets, we propose two novel animal Re-ID methods. BIFOR (Background Invariant Feature extractOR) is a three-step training procedure based on a novel mini-batch sampling strategy, designed to mitigate the covariate shift inherent to generalizable Re-ID, where a network trained on a source dataset must perform on an unknown target domain. We then present PAW-ViT (Part-AWare animal re-identification Vision Transformer), that replaces the standard classification token of a ViT with K learnable part tokens. Each additional token specializes into a specific anatomical region of the animal via semantic segmentation knowledge distillation, in a multi-task learning framework. The part embeddings are then fused by an additional aggregation token into a single part-aware descriptor. Both methods outperform SOTA baselines, particularly in uncontrolled cross-camera scenarios. PAW-ViT further confirms the broader applicability of our framework to quadrupeds, achieving SOTA results on datasets such as ATRW (Amur tigers) and YakReID-103 (yaks).

Finally, we introduce a novel benchmark on SEAdogSEA-Indonesia evaluating the ability of SOTA Re-ID methods to operate in real-world, long-term settings where individuals and environments evolve across years, including an open-set protocol that requires recognizing previously unseen individuals. The benchmark covers the complete pipeline, from raw camera-trap videos to contact networks, providing a realistic setup for downstream use by ecologists and epidemiologists.
